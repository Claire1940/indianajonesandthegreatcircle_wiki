<Section icon={Sparkles} title="The Secret of Secrets: Sistine Chapel Puzzle">

As you delve deeper into the Vatican, you will eventually gain access to the back offices of the Sistine Chapel. This area houses one of the more complex puzzles in the game, tied to the "Secret of Secrets" mystery. To unlock this safe, you cannot simply find a number written on a wall; you must engage with the history of the saints and the stars.

On the table in the back office, you will find a note instructing you to inspect the statues of **Peter with the Key** and **Paul with the Sword**. These figures aren't just for decoration. Picking them up and turning them over reveals astronomical symbols representing specific planets. Once you have these symbols, you must locate the planetary chart in the same room.

<Steps>
  <Step num="1" title="Inspect the Statues">
    Find Peter and Paul on the side tables. Rotate them to find the planetary symbols etched into their bases.
  </Step>
  <Step num="2" title="Consult the Planetary Chart">
    Locate the large chart on the wall. Align the symbols you found with the numbers corresponding to their distance from the Sun.
  </Step>
  <Step num="3" title="Input the Code">
    The resulting calculation leads to the code **4471**. Input this into the office safe to claim your prize.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Callout type="warning" title="Important">
The planetary chart logic relies on the order of planets from the Sun. If you are unfamiliar with basic astronomy, double-check the chart's legend in the room.
</Callout>

<Section icon={Shield} title="Fieldwork Safes: Savage Discovery and A Nun in Trouble">

Several safes are locked behind specific Fieldwork missions that trigger as you explore the Vatican. "A Savage Discovery" and "A Nun in Trouble" are two primary side activities that lead Indy to high-security areas containing locked chests and safes. These missions often require finding specific keys before you can even attempt to crack the safe codes.

In "A Savage Discovery," you are tasked with investigating an excavation site. After neutralizing the guards and obtaining the **Cellar Key**, you can enter the basement beneath the post office near the Tower of Nicholas. Inside, a guard carries a note mentioning "November last year." Given the game's setting in 1937, "last year" refers to 1936, and November is the 11th month.

For the "A Nun in Trouble" safe in the Museum Garden, the solution is much more abstract. You must find the **Secret Delivery Letter**, which notes a package arrival on October 15th. Cross-referencing this with the in-game calendar reveals the day was a Friday. Looking at the "Dice" code on that specific calendar day yields the final combination.

<Section icon={Swords} title="The Father and Son Mystery: Borgia Tower">

The "Father and Son" mystery is one of the final puzzles you will solve in the Vatican district. It becomes available only after you have successfully completed the "A Nun in Trouble" mission and received **Juliana's Key**. This key opens a side room near the Borgia Tower, where Indy finds a room occupied by a cat, a dog, and a very cryptic poem.

To solve this, you must loot the poem and look for circled letters: **I, R, I, A, T, A, S, O**. These letters must then be applied to a square diagram found in the room. Each letter or combination of letters corresponds to a specific numerical value based on its position in the grid.

| Letter Sequence | Grid Result | Final Code |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| I R | 5 | **5** |
| I A | 8 | **8** |
| T A | 7 | **7** |
| S O | 3 | **3** |

This safe is particularly rewarding because it represents the culmination of several interlocking side quests. The square diagram logic is a recurring theme in the game's later stages, so mastering it here will help you in Giza and beyond.

Unlike the previous puzzles, the code for this safe is actually hidden in plain sight if you read the documentation carefully. At the very bottom of the "Gina's Request" note, the year **1891** is mentioned in a context that suggests it serves as a secondary security measure.
